Every organization has one. The engineer who answers pages at 3am. The operator who "just knows" how to fix the mysterious database issue. The hero.

They are celebrated. They are indispensable. They are a symptom of systemic failure.

## The Heroism Trap

When someone consistently saves the day, two things happen simultaneously. First, the organization develops a dependency on that individual. Second, the underlying problems that require heroism never get fixed.

> The presence of a hero is evidence that the system cannot sustain itself.

This creates a feedback loop. The hero accumulates knowledge. The knowledge stays in their head. The system becomes more fragile. More heroism is required.

## Measuring the Real Cost

Organizations rarely calculate the true cost of operational heroism:

- **Attention debt** - Every interrupt fragments focus across the team
- **Knowledge silos** - Critical information exists only in one person's memory
- **Learned helplessness** - Others stop developing competence because "someone will handle it"
- **Burnout trajectory** - Heroes eventually leave, taking everything with them

The departure of a hero is not a personnel problem. It is an infrastructure event.

## Breaking the Cycle

The solution is not to punish heroism. It is to make heroism unnecessary.

This requires deliberate effort:

1. Document every intervention immediately after it happens
2. Treat each incident as a system defect, not a solved problem
3. Rotate on-call responsibilities even when it feels inefficient
4. Measure time-to-resolution by the second responder, not the first

The goal is boredom. When operations become predictable, heroes become unnecessary.
